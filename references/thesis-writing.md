# Thesis And Proposal Writing

Use this file for thesis structures, proposal outlines, chapter plans, and academic writing revision.

## Standard Structure

1. Introduction
2. Literature review
3. Theory / causal explanation
4. Research design and method
5. Empirical analysis
6. Conclusion

Every section should earn its place by helping answer the research question.
When the draft feels long but not persuasive, diagnose whether sections are
describing background instead of advancing the argument.

## Introduction

Include:

- research puzzle;
- research question;
- why it matters;
- core argument;
- method and evidence preview;
- contribution;
- chapter roadmap.

Do not begin with broad historical background unless it directly creates the puzzle.

## Introduction Diagnostic

The introduction is ready only when a reader can answer:

- What is the phenomenon or pattern?
- Why is it puzzling rather than merely interesting?
- What is the exact research question?
- What is the author's short answer?
- What evidence and method will be used?
- Which debate or research problem does the project change?

Common errors:

- Starting with a very broad history of international relations.
- Announcing a topic but not a puzzle.
- Asking several questions without a main question.
- Giving a policy opinion before building an academic argument.
- Hiding the answer until the conclusion.

Opening pattern:

```text
Existing expectations suggest [expected pattern]. Yet in [case/time], we observe
[surprising pattern]. This raises the question: [research question]. I argue
that [X affects Y through mechanism M under condition S]. I test this claim
using [evidence/method], and show that [contribution].
```

## Literature Review

The literature review should create a debate map, not a bibliography parade.

Organize by:

- explanation type;
- mechanism;
- method;
- evidence;
- scope condition;
- unresolved gap.

End by showing exactly where the user's project enters the debate.

Common errors:

- Listing authors one by one without grouping explanations.
- Summarizing every source equally instead of using the literature to build the
  research problem.
- Treating "few scholars have studied this case" as the whole gap.
- Criticizing prior studies without identifying what your project can test or
  clarify.
- Inventing literature when the user has not provided sources.

Review paragraph pattern:

```text
One group explains Y through [explanation type]. This view highlights [useful
insight], but it has difficulty explaining [anomaly/scope/problem]. A second
group emphasizes [another explanation]. It improves on [point], yet leaves open
[gap]. This project builds on [strength] and tests whether [your mechanism]
explains [specified outcome].
```

## Theory Section

Include:

- concept definitions;
- causal diagram or mechanism chain;
- assumptions;
- hypotheses;
- scope conditions;
- alternative explanations.

The theory section should produce testable hypotheses.

Theory diagnostic:

- Are the concepts defined before the mechanism begins?
- Does the mechanism identify actors, activities, and sequence?
- Does each step imply observable evidence?
- Are scope conditions stated when the argument is bounded?
- Are rival explanations acknowledged in a way that can be tested?
- Do the hypotheses actually follow from the mechanism?

Common errors:

- Repeating literature instead of stating the user's own explanation.
- Jumping from X to Y without a mechanism.
- Using "influence" or "pressure" as a black box.
- Making a universal claim when the argument only works under specific
  institutional, temporal, or actor-level conditions.
- Adding variables because they sound important, not because the theory needs
  them.

## Method Section

Include:

- case or sample selection;
- variable definitions;
- operationalization and measurement;
- data sources;
- testing strategy;
- control strategy;
- limitations.

Method diagnostic:

- Does the method match the type of question?
- Does the case/sample selection help evaluate the hypothesis?
- Are X, Y, mediator, confounders, and possible colliders separated?
- Are operational definitions and data sources specific enough to replicate?
- Does the design say what evidence would weaken the claim?

Common errors:

- Choosing cases only because data are easy to find.
- Calling a narrative "process tracing" without testing mechanism steps.
- Calling a comparison "controlled" without explaining which variables vary and
  which are held constant.
- Adding many controls without a causal diagram.
- Treating limitations as a formality instead of a boundary on inference.

## Empirical Chapters

Organize evidence around hypotheses or mechanism steps, not around a simple timeline unless the timeline itself tests the mechanism.

Each empirical section should state:

- what claim it tests;
- what evidence is used;
- what alternative explanation is considered;
- what the evidence supports or weakens.

Empirical chapter pattern:

```text
This section tests [hypothesis/mechanism step]. If the argument is correct, we
should observe [observable implication]. The evidence shows [finding]. This
matters because [link back to mechanism]. A rival explanation would expect
[alternative implication], but [evidence comparison].
```

Common errors:

- Writing a chronological story that never returns to the hypothesis.
- Treating every interesting fact as evidence.
- Using evidence that appears after the outcome to explain the outcome.
- Ignoring negative or ambiguous evidence.
- Ending sections without an inferential takeaway.

## Conclusion

Include:

- direct answer to the research question;
- theoretical contribution;
- empirical contribution;
- policy implication if relevant;
- limitations;
- next research steps.

Conclusion diagnostic:

- Does it answer the exact research question from the introduction?
- Does it distinguish what was shown from what remains uncertain?
- Does it state theoretical and empirical contribution separately?
- Does it avoid introducing a new major argument at the end?
- If policy implications are included, are they grounded in the findings?

## Proposal Outline

| Section | Content |
|---|---|
| Title | Clear topic, cases, and outcome. |
| Puzzle | Why the case or pattern is not obvious. |
| Research question | One main question. |
| Literature review | Debate map and gap. |
| Theory | Causal mechanism and hypotheses. |
| Research design | Cases/data, variables, operationalization, method. |
| Expected contribution | What knowledge the project adds. |
| Work plan | Evidence collection and writing schedule. |

## Revision Checklist

- Every section has a role in answering the research question.
- The same key terms are used consistently across chapters.
- The literature review creates the problem that the theory answers.
- Hypotheses appear before evidence is evaluated.
- Method choices are justified, not merely named.
- Empirical evidence is organized by claim, hypothesis, or mechanism step.
- The conclusion does not overclaim beyond the evidence.
