# Reasoning Patterns

Use this file to reason from a broad international-relations or political-science topic to a researchable design.

Examples are not templates to copy. They demonstrate reasoning moves:

- narrow a broad topic;
- identify possible outcomes Y;
- find variation, contrast, anomaly, or change;
- ask an answerable research question;
- build a mechanism from the user's own actors and context;
- name evidence that could test the claim.

Do not reuse from examples:

- substantive causal mechanisms;
- case choices;
- hypotheses;
- variable lists;
- theory labels;
- empirical claims.

Core rule:

```text
Copy the reasoning move, never the substantive mechanism.
```

## Pattern 1: Broad Topic -> Generate Candidate Outcomes

Bad move:

```text
Immediately convert a broad topic into a familiar research question.
```

Problem:

The outcome is usually still vague. The agent may import a familiar frame, actor, case, or strategic posture that the user did not provide.

Better move:

Generate candidate outcomes from the user's own wording and context. Do not use a fixed outcome list.

Use these as diagnostic dimensions, not answer choices:

- What actor behavior could vary?
- What decision, practice, rule, claim, interaction, or resource could change?
- What observable event, degree, direction, timing, sequence, or frequency could be measured?
- What comparison is implied by the user's topic: across cases, within one case over time, across institutions, across issue areas, or across episodes?
- What evidence would make each candidate outcome observable?

Then ask which generated outcome best matches the user's intent.

Do not choose a cause, case, theory family, or mechanism until the outcome and scope are fixed.

## Pattern 2: Topic -> Variation

Weak topic:

```text
[broad theme or field label]
```

Better move:

Generate the variation from the user's own topic instead of supplying a sample outcome.

Ask:

- What result, behavior, relation, or condition in the user's wording could vary?
- Does it vary across cases, time, actors, institutions, issue areas, or episodes?
- Is the variation about occurrence, degree, timing, direction, sequence, or meaning?
- What evidence would show that the variation exists?

Reasoning move:

Find variation in occurrence, degree, timing, success/failure, or cross-case difference.

## Pattern 2B: Generate Topic-Specific Question Forms

For broad topics, generate several topic-specific question forms from the user's phenomenon. Do not present a fixed menu of question families.

Use this worksheet internally:

| Diagnostic dimension | Question to ask before writing |
|---|---|
| Difference | What varies, and across what comparison set? |
| Process | What process is unknown, and what traces could reveal it? |
| Boundary | Where might the explanation apply or fail? |
| Timing | What changed before and after a specific period or event? |
| Comparison | Which comparison would make the puzzle clearer? |

After using the worksheet, write fresh question formulations in the user's vocabulary. Do not copy the diagnostic dimension names into the final question unless they are genuinely useful.

## Pattern 3: Mechanism Must Be Rebuilt

Do not start from a favorite theory or from an example mechanism.

Start from:

1. Who are the actors?
2. What changed in their incentives, beliefs, resources, information, or constraints?
3. What action did that change make more likely?
4. How did that action alter interaction, bargaining, institutions, or distribution?
5. How did that produce the outcome Y?
6. What traces would each step leave?

Only after answering these should you name a theory family.

## Pattern 4: Literature Review As Debate Map

Weak move:

```text
Author A says...
Author B says...
Author C says...
```

Better move:

Group user-provided sources by explanation type, mechanism, method, evidence, and limitation.

Possible groups:

- power-structure explanations;
- domestic-politics explanations;
- institutional explanations;
- ideas, identity, or perception explanations;
- economic interdependence explanations;
- historical path-dependence explanations;
- method or evidence disagreements.

Do not invent sources. If the user has not provided literature, ask for titles, abstracts, notes, or bibliographies, or use a separate literature-search workflow only if explicitly requested.

## Pattern 5: Draft Diagnosis

When the user provides a draft, do not rewrite everything immediately.

Diagnose the weakest link:

```text
topic -> research question -> literature-review design -> causal explanation -> hypotheses -> variables -> operationalization -> testing -> thesis structure
```

Then revise the weakest link first.

## Anti-Copy Checklist

Before producing a mechanism, hypothesis, or variable table, check:

- Did the outcome Y come from the user's topic rather than an example?
- Are the actors and scope named by the user or clearly inferred from the user's topic?
- Is the mechanism rebuilt from the current case context?
- Are examples used only to demonstrate the type of reasoning move?
- Did the answer avoid importing case-specific claims from unrelated examples?
