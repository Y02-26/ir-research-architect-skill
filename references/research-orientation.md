# Research Orientation

Use this file when the user needs to clarify what kind of research they are doing and what standards should guide the project.

## Academic Research Vs Policy Research

| Dimension | Academic research | Policy research |
|---|---|---|
| Main goal | Produce reliable knowledge and theoretical contribution. | Help decision-makers understand options and consequences. |
| Core question | Why or how does a phenomenon occur? | What should be done, with what trade-offs? |
| Evaluation | Clarity, evidence, inference, contribution, transparency. | Relevance, timeliness, feasibility, risk assessment. |
| Output | Thesis, article, research proposal. | Policy memo, option paper, briefing, risk assessment. |

Many projects mix both. If so, separate the academic question from the policy implication.

## Orientation Diagnostic

Ask the user these questions when the project is still blurry:

- Is the user trying to explain a phenomenon, evaluate a policy, or both?
- Who is the intended reader: examiner, journal reviewer, decision-maker, or
  general audience?
- Does the output need a causal argument, a descriptive map, a risk assessment,
  or a recommendation?
- What would count as a successful answer?
- Is the project judged mainly by theoretical contribution, practical relevance,
  empirical accuracy, or decision usefulness?

If the project mixes academic and policy purposes, separate the layers:

| Layer | Question form | Output |
|---|---|---|
| Academic | Why/how did Y occur? Under what conditions does X affect Y? | Explanation, hypothesis, evidence, contribution. |
| Policy | What should actor A do, given constraints and trade-offs? | Options, risks, feasibility, recommendation. |
| Bridge | What does the explanation imply for policy choices? | Conditional implication, not a direct command. |

## Scientific Research Logic

Scientific research should:

- ask a clear question;
- define concepts;
- state causal or descriptive claims precisely;
- connect claims to evidence;
- make assumptions and limits explicit;
- allow other researchers to evaluate or challenge the inference.

The agent should keep the user from sliding into commentary. A research design
is not only "having views"; it is a transparent chain from question to concept,
claim, evidence, inference, and limitation.

## Quality Standards

A strong IR research design is:

- **clear**: key terms, cases, time frame, and outcome are specified;
- **meaningful**: it responds to a puzzle, debate, anomaly, or important real-world problem;
- **answerable**: data or evidence can plausibly be collected;
- **testable**: hypotheses or claims can be supported or weakened by evidence;
- **bounded**: the argument states where it applies and where it may not;
- **honest**: uncertainty, limitations, and rival explanations are not hidden.

## Limits Of IR Research

IR research often faces:

- limited ability to control variables;
- complex interaction between domestic, international, and individual-level factors;
- scarce or biased evidence;
- difficulty observing intentions and decision processes;
- historically unique events;
- ethical and access constraints.

Do not respond to these limits by becoming vague. Respond by narrowing the question, improving comparison, tracing mechanisms, and stating uncertainty.

## Common Orientation Errors

- Treating a broad field, such as "China-US relations", as a research question.
- Asking "what do you think about..." when the assignment requires causal
  analysis.
- Mixing normative judgment with empirical explanation without marking the
  difference.
- Choosing a fashionable topic before identifying observable variation or a
  puzzle.
- Assuming that policy relevance automatically creates academic contribution.
- Treating lack of literature on one case as enough to justify the project.
- Making the research question so broad that evidence collection becomes
  impossible.

## Repair Moves

| Problem | Repair |
|---|---|
| Topic is too broad | Specify actor, outcome, time, place, and variation. |
| Question is only descriptive | Add a why/how/under what conditions layer if explanation is required. |
| Question is policy-only | Separate explanation from recommendation. |
| No puzzle | Compare observed pattern with expectation, theory, or another case. |
| No evidence path | Narrow the outcome or choose observable indicators. |
| Too many goals | Choose one main question and put secondary issues in scope conditions or limitations. |
